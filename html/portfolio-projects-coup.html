PROJECT

Coup, drive conversion to the valuation journey

UX Research
UX Design

SUMMARY

I lead the UX research and UX process of the Coup project.

PROBLEM STATEMENT

We’re Coup, the faff-free service that makes finding, buying and selling a used car - easy

Selling a car can be frustrating, time-consuming and hard work. On top of that, having bad UX for users to valuate and leading to sell their car will make situations worst. I believe a user journey from start to finish should be easy, fast and seamless for a great UX. So that users won’t have to think, is the successful mission for every user flow on any website.

Currently, the Coup website displays the valuation banner poorly towards the bottom of the homepage that results to users struggle to find where they can get a valuation for their car. I gathered this evidence from the usability testing lab session I have conducted with my squad and from data recordings. The majority of users struggled to find this feature to help them sell their car or just to get a valuation.

How can I help users to find where they can get a valuation for their car that leads to selling their car from the homepage?

PAIN POINTS - ASSUMPTIONS

Selling a car in general
My feeling on the way how people sell their cars can be a very painful process, the long process of selling privately i.e advertising, viewing, interacting etc. Part-exchanging for less money and being under pressure. Selling to third party companies could potentially be much less with poor customer experience.

Selling a car on Coup
User experience could be improved a whole lot more to help users through the process of getting a valuation and selling their car. The valuation feature isn't clear and prominent on the current homepage especially on mobile.

WHAT IF?

I ask myself what if I make the process that much easier and a better experience, would that encourage users to change their car more often? What are people’s general feelings about selling their car? What is the process of selling their car? What do people think of selling their cars to third party companies? These are some of the questions I needed validations.

DEFINE GOALS

By defining the goals, will help me find out what I am trying to do and learn. This is where I will reflect on the notes from the interviews and usability testing to collect data that answer the questions.

I want to discover:
The users’ pain points, needs, and goals when selling a car
The users’ processes when selling a car
How users currently using the website to value their car
What users think Coup offers them (to see if the basic concept of the site is clear)
What do users think about selling their car to a third party company

FURTHER UNDERSTANDING

Who do I need to learn this from?
To be able to understand this, I will need to speak to users who had experience, who are in the process of selling their car and thinking of selling their car

How will I use the data I've gathered?
To identify the pain points users have when going through the valuation process and to find the solutions to make the process a greater user experience so that users have the confidence to move forward in either selling their car or to browse cars on the website.

OKRs (OJECTIVE KEY RESULTS)

Increase conversion– sell over 50 cars

Increase registration – returning users

USER STORY

I have put together a user story with the goal in mind, that’s to make the process of getting a valuation on a car a much more enjoyable and delightful process. This is also to help me refer back to the problems I am trying to solve.

As a user, I would like…
A way to help me get a valuation and sell my car easily without the long and painful process…
So that I can save time and money, have the confidence to search for a new car knowing that I can sell my car easily in the future and stress-free.

UX RESEARCH

User interview - The purpose
User interviews will help me understand what the user think and feels, to empathise with the users. This gives myself a bigger picture to identify the pain points and map out the requirements for the feature(s). It also helps me to understand the emotions and body language that adds more details to the research.

Usability testing - The purpose
The purpose of this session is to find the problems users encounter when they are going through the selling process and to find out further how they feel about it. Creating a list of scenarios for users to do. These will reveal important information relevant to the subject we are testing.

With the script planned, I asked participants to look around the homepage and see what they make of it. So, what they think they could do on here, what the site is and who’s the site is for and so on. I also asked participants to complete different tasks of how they might go through the valuation journey. Whether it’s helping a family member to get a valuation of their car, a recommendation, and applying damage for a more accurate valuation. The scenarios will follow up with multiple-choice questions.

Data Recordings
I have added tracking to record the current flow of the valuation journey using Hotjar, to identify how users interact with it and to understand what I can learn from the recordings.

Usability testing - Homepage banner
From the research gathered, it has identified the pain points many users have going through the flow. Some would decide to leave after several attempts within the process. Content and flow of the valuation journey is confusing on mobile (according to data, 80% of the users browse the coup website on mobile, therefore I have focused on the mobile first approach), has proven most difficult to use.

All users went to the hamburger navigation menu to start the valuation process which took some time to find except for 1 user. On mobile the valuation feature is hidden behind the hamburger menu (as well as another banner towards the bottom of the page) therefore, it will require users to tap twice to find the feature hidden behind the menu, and to scroll down the page to see the ofter banner that isn't visible.

The ‘What’s my car worth?’ (valuation banner) copy isn’t clear and the banner doesn’t stand out enough. From the findings, some users saw the banner but didn’t click on it and due to the inconsistency of copy within the banner, they weren’t sure if it was to valuate, sell or just finding out how much your car is worth.

Usability testing - Valuation process
The majority of testers have successfully completed the scenarios going through the journey, however most of the testers got stuck on the last page (valuation) trying to book an appointment to sell their car. Some would have gave up at that point. Learning how testers use the website has helped me gained valuable insight to analyse the data.

Summary
This has validated my initial assumptions of pain points users have when getting a valuation for their car. From the findings, the usability issues are clear. The homepage banner needed to be incorporated above the fold somehow, and the focus on the valuation process is on the last page and not the whole journey.

After analysing the data from the UX research lab, I facilitated a workshop with the squad and have mapped out the diagram below showing the progress, identified the pain points from conducting the user interviews and usability testing. Ready to move onto the next steps of build, measure and learn.

HYPOTHESIS

If we… allow customers to find the valuation feature and make the user flow combining with registration a much better, quicker and easier user experience…
Then… we increase customer experience, easier access and reduce drop-off to the valuation journey…
So that… conversion and registration is increased and customers see Coup as the convenient companion when getting a valuation on their car. This will result to returning customers and tell others about Coup.

DESIGN SPRINT

The front end team within the squad will be working on the quick fixes to improve the current experience that was identified during the usability testing session.

As for the next steps, I will facilitate a 1 week design sprint to work towards the new and improved experience, using the structure of the research I gathered. The five days will involve defining hypothesis, research (A/B test data, heatmaps, competitor analysis, current data, user tests etc), ideation, prototyping, script planning, remote/lab testing, insight analysis, present ﬁndings and iteration.

This will help me to achieve my goals and get the MVP out there to get it to the hands of users, and gather feedback from it. Then iterate from there.

I asked the squad to come into the Monday’s workshop with research and each present research of the websites they have chosen. To talk about what’s cool, how might we do it better, what’s not cool, etc. With the question in mind ‘What’s the big idea here that might be useful?’ We then went to sketch out components that we liked from the research and annotated them for reference.

With the research gathered from each person, the squad went away sketching out ideas and regrouped to talk about the sketches. Then we further defined the ideas with the Crazy 8’s method and each person voted on what they think will produce the best outcomes on hitting the OKRs.

WIREFRAMES

homepage banner
During the workshop, the squad voted on the best ideas and I took the chosen sketches to ideate further digitally. Mocking up three versions of how to best place the valuation banner above the fold, I decided to get the other squads from the wider business to dot vote what they think works best. Sticking the wireframes in a meeting room wall, I provided the teams with dot stickers, post-it notes and sharpies to ask questions.

The other squads voted and provided feedback on the three ideas. Therefore, I went away to further iterate and did another round of voting. This time, I wanted to get thoughts around copy within the banner feature.

Valuation page
Myself and the squad prioritised the outcomes and came up with many solutions to the problems users have navigating through the valuation process. The usability testing results have identified that the UX could be improved with more sign post messaging to guide the user through to the final destination. Agreeing as a team, we narrowed the solutions down for the MVP and turned the problems into questions for how we can solve them. I decided to frame the problems into How Might We questions.

1. How Might We… Tell people how long the process will take?
2. How Might We… Show them how many stages there are and how many are left?
3. How Might We… Make the valuation page much easier to understand?

As part of the design sprint, I conducted usability testing on the valuation page because it has many iterations and features added (from the feedback in the research lab), I wanted to quickly test the waters and gather feedback to iterate further.

Presenting the results to the squad, it shows the majority of users successfully completed each task except for two users on one of the tasks. The overall usability score is 91 that shows users are interacting with the features. It has helped me identified the solutions are effective and to see how the prototype has helped users’ needs. The storybook library

Using Storybook will allow myself and the teams to design and build UI components effectively and consistency. With the patterns and components that make up the brand of the product, piecing the UI components together for hi-fidelity designs made it easier.

HI-FIDELITY DESIGN

Stitching the UI components together to see how they compromise and feel. This creates opportunity to refine further ready for development. I will be A/B testing the designs with variations to get feedback and iterate further.

Homepage banner
Introducing the new valuation banner above the fold will increase visibility to guide users to the valuation process. Changing the CTA copy from ‘I know what I want’ to ‘Find cars’ will make it easier to understand what that call to action is. Also, distinguishing the CTA buttons between the ‘Find cars’ and ‘help me choose’ to prioritise and extinguish the CTAs.

Valuation page
Working together as a squad, we broke down the design into tickets accordingly, to help prioritise the tasks in greater detail. With further iterations made, I am confident to put this into build and test further as well as tracking the data to further iterate and validate.

Features implemented
1. Progress bar: Show how many stages there are and how many are left
2. Indicate how long the process will take
3. Edit inside valuation box: Clearly show users where to edit their car details to update their valuation
4. Add assumptions accordion and move under valuation
5. Add two CTA buttons to guide users to sell their car and to guide users to view the cars we have for sale.
6. Don’t show the booking an appointment feature until ‘Book to sell your car’ CTA button has been selected.
7. Desktop should be the same as mobile with one central column and the same button functionality.

Key Learnings

Importance of Communication

Communication is an important part of good design and no matter how amazing the product is, if people can't use it then they will simply leave or stop using the product. The challenge is to use human-centred design to create great results, the 'feature' is to improve the user experience so that customers are able to find at a glace and add to enjoyment. The main objective is to make the feature searchable, successful and that customers won't have to think and return back to the site.
